# Bridging the Occupancy-Behavioral Divide: A Federated Transformer Framework for Context-Aware Energy Optimization in Shared Spaces

**Abstract** — The efficient management of energy resources in shared indoor environments remains a persistent challenge, often relying on coarse occupancy prediction models that fail to capture the nuanced, dynamic nature of human activity. Existing approaches, while achieving high accuracy in determining presence, fall short in inferring the qualitative aspects of occupancy that significantly influence energy consumption and occupant comfort. This paper addresses these limitations by proposing a novel privacy-preserving Federated Learning (FL) framework. This framework leverages Transformer-based anomaly detection on anonymized sensor streams to move beyond generic occupancy prediction. Our core hypothesis is that by inferring localized, emergent behavioral "moods" and "activity intensities," we can achieve dynamic, context-aware energy optimization. This approach aims to minimize energy waste and enhance occupant comfort without requiring explicit individual user profiling or intrusive data collection. By integrating advanced deep learning architectures with robust privacy-preserving techniques, we posit a system capable of understanding the subtle shifts in collective behavior, thereby enabling a more intelligent and adaptive energy management strategy for shared spaces. The proposed methodology directly confronts the privacy concerns associated with granular user data by focusing on aggregate, anonymized signals and learning distributedly across edge devices. This research aims to establish a new paradigm for intelligent building automation, moving from simple presence detection to a deeper, context-aware understanding of occupant dynamics.

## I. Introduction

The increasing urbanization and the proliferation of shared indoor spaces, ranging from offices and educational institutions to public transportation hubs and retail environments, have amplified the imperative for intelligent and efficient energy management systems. Traditional approaches to controlling building services such as lighting, heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) have predominantly relied on static scheduling, manual overrides, or rudimentary occupancy detection mechanisms. While these methods offer a baseline level of automation, they are inherently inefficient, often leading to significant energy wastage due to over-conditioning or suboptimal operation when spaces are underutilized or occupied by individuals with differing thermal or lighting preferences. The advent of the Internet of Things (IoT) and ubiquitous sensing technologies has paved the way for more sophisticated solutions. Researchers have made significant strides in developing predictive models for occupancy, utilizing a diverse array of sensors including Passive Infrared (PIR) motion detectors, light sensors (LDRs), temperature sensors, and even CO2 sensors to infer the presence and number of occupants. These models, often employing machine learning techniques such as Support Vector Machines (SVMs), Random Forests, and more recently, Deep Neural Networks like Feedforward Neural Networks (FNNs), have demonstrated considerable success in achieving high prediction accuracy for simple presence detection and even forecasting occupancy trends over short durations.

However, a critical gap persists in bridging the chasm between simple occupancy detection and truly intelligent, context-aware energy optimization. The current generation of systems, despite their advancements in predicting *if* a space is occupied and *how many* people are present, largely fail to account for the qualitative aspects of occupancy. The nature of activity within a space can vary dramatically, from a quiet, focused work session to a dynamic, collaborative meeting, or a relaxed social gathering. Each of these scenarios imposes different demands on the building's environmental control systems. A space filled with individuals engaged in sedentary, low-intensity work might require less ventilation and ambient lighting compared to a space hosting an energetic group discussion. Furthermore, the collective psychological state, or "mood," of occupants can influence their perception of comfort and their tolerance for minor deviations in environmental parameters. Existing systems, by treating all occupancy as a uniform state, are incapable of adapting to these nuanced differences. This lack of granular understanding leads to a suboptimal application of energy resources, either by over-providing conditioned air or light when not required, or conversely, by failing to adequately meet occupant needs during periods of higher activity intensity, thereby compromising comfort and productivity.

The limitations of current methods are further compounded by growing concerns surrounding data privacy and security. Many advanced personalization techniques or highly granular sensing strategies, while theoretically capable of capturing richer contextual information, necessitate the collection and processing of sensitive individual-level data. This raises ethical dilemmas and legal challenges, particularly in shared public or semi-public spaces where explicit user consent for detailed tracking can be difficult to obtain and manage. The desire for personalized comfort and efficient energy use must be balanced against the fundamental right to privacy. Traditional centralized cloud-based learning architectures, even when attempting anonymization, can be vulnerable to data aggregation attacks or sophisticated re-identification techniques, especially as the volume and variety of sensor data increase. The inherent trust required in a central authority to handle and protect sensitive data presents a significant barrier to widespread adoption. Therefore, there is a pressing need for a paradigm shift towards systems that can extract meaningful, actionable insights from ambient environmental data while rigorously preserving user privacy, moving beyond mere detection to genuine contextual understanding.

## II. Problem Statement

The pervasive challenge in current building energy management systems lies in their inability to transcend the binary and coarse-grained understanding of human presence to achieve dynamic, context-aware optimization. The state-of-the-art primarily focuses on predicting *whether* a space is occupied and *how many* individuals are present. While achieving high accuracies (e.g., 98% in occupancy prediction as reported in some research) is a significant technical feat, this metric alone is insufficient for optimizing energy consumption in a manner that genuinely enhances occupant comfort and minimizes waste. The fundamental limitation stems from the assumption that occupancy, irrespective of its nature, imposes uniform energy demands. This assumption is demonstrably false in real-world scenarios. For instance, a lecture hall with 50 students attentively listening to a professor will have vastly different thermal and lighting requirements than a similar hall filled with 50 students engaged in a lively group debate. Similarly, a co-working space where individuals are engaged in deep, solitary work will have different environmental needs than one hosting a collaborative brainstorming session. The current methodologies, by treating all forms of occupancy equally, are inherently inefficient. They often default to over-provisioning resources to ensure comfort in worst-case scenarios, leading to substantial energy waste when occupancy is present but less demanding. Conversely, they may under-provision during periods of high activity intensity if the system's prediction model fails to account for the increased metabolic heat or lighting needs.

Furthermore, the problem is exacerbated by the lack of mechanisms to infer the subtle, emergent, and localized behavioral states of occupants. Human activity within shared spaces is not static; it is fluid and dynamic, influenced by a myriad of factors including task type, social interaction, time of day, and even the collective psychological atmosphere. These dynamic states, which can be broadly categorized as "moods" and "activity intensities," directly correlate with energy demands. A "low mood" might correlate with decreased physical movement and a greater preference for warmer temperatures, while a high "activity intensity" could signify increased metabolic heat generation, greater movement, and potentially a need for enhanced ventilation or adjusted lighting. Current systems are ill-equipped to detect or interpret these subtle signals. The data collected by typical environmental sensors (PIR, LDR, temperature) is often treated as a simple indicator of presence rather than a rich source of information about the *nature* of occupancy. This disconnect prevents the system from adapting its energy management strategies in real-time to reflect the actual, and often evolving, needs of the occupants.

Adding another layer of complexity to this problem is the paramount importance of data privacy and the inherent limitations of traditional centralized data processing approaches. As we seek to gain deeper insights into occupant behavior to drive more sophisticated energy optimization, the potential for collecting granular, individual-level data becomes a significant concern. Explicit user profiling, detailed tracking of individual movements, or the use of intrusive sensing technologies raise ethical red flags and can lead to user resistance and legal ramifications. Moreover, centralized cloud-based learning, even with anonymization techniques, can be vulnerable. The aggregation of vast amounts of data, even if ostensibly anonymized, can still be susceptible to re-identification attacks or inferential breaches, especially when combined with advanced analytical capabilities. The risk of unintended privacy violations, even in the pursuit of efficiency, is substantial. Therefore, a robust solution must not only achieve advanced contextual understanding but also fundamentally adhere to privacy-preserving principles, ensuring that individual users are not profiled or compromised in the process. The current research landscape often presents a dichotomy: either sacrifice granular behavioral insights for privacy or compromise privacy for potentially richer, but riskier, data. There is a critical need to find a synthesis that offers both.

## III. Objectives

The overarching goal of this research is to develop and validate a novel energy optimization framework that achieves superior efficiency and occupant comfort in shared indoor spaces by moving beyond generic occupancy prediction to infer localized, emergent behavioral states. To this end, we establish the following specific objectives:

1.  **Develop a Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning (FL) Architecture:** To design and implement a robust FL framework capable of training sophisticated machine learning models collaboratively across multiple edge devices without centralizing raw, sensitive sensor data. This architecture must ensure that model updates, not the data itself, are shared, thereby mitigating privacy risks associated with data aggregation and transfer. The FL setup will be crucial for enabling distributed learning on anonymized sensor streams at the edge.
    *   **Success Metric:** Successful deployment and operation of an FL system where multiple edge nodes contribute to model training and convergence is achieved without any direct data sharing from individual nodes to a central server beyond aggregated model parameters.

2.  **Implement Transformer-Based Anomaly Detection for Behavioral Inference:** To leverage the power of Transformer architectures for analyzing sequential, anonymized sensor data streams to detect anomalies that are indicative of shifts in localized occupant behavior. Specifically, the objective is to train Transformers to identify patterns that correlate with emergent "moods" and varying "activity intensities" within shared spaces. This moves beyond simple signal thresholding to understanding temporal dynamics.
    *   **Success Metric:** Demonstration of a statistically significant correlation between detected anomalies by the Transformer model and qualitative human observations of changing activity levels or perceived collective moods within controlled experimental environments.

3.  **Achieve Dynamic, Context-Aware Energy Optimization:** To translate the inferred behavioral states (moods and activity intensities) into actionable energy management strategies that dynamically adjust environmental controls (lighting, HVAC). This optimization must be "context-aware," meaning it adapts to the specific inferred state of the occupants, thereby transcending the limitations of static, occupancy-based control. The system should actively minimize energy consumption by matching resource provision to actual, nuanced demand.
    *   **Success Metric:** Quantifiable reduction in energy consumption (e.g., kWh) for lighting and HVAC systems in experimental spaces under the proposed FL-Transformer framework compared to baseline occupancy-prediction-based control and unmanaged scenarios, while maintaining or improving occupant comfort scores.

4.  **Ensure Robust Privacy Protection of Anonymized Sensor Streams:** To rigorously ensure that the proposed system does not require or lead to the explicit profiling or identification of individual users. This includes employing advanced anonymization techniques on sensor data at the source and designing the FL and Transformer models such that they learn from aggregate, emergent patterns rather than individual signatures. The system must inherently protect against re-identification risks, even from subtle temporal correlations.
    *   **Success Metric:** Absence of any identifiable user data within the training process or model outputs. Independent security audit demonstrating that no individual user's presence or specific behavioral patterns can be inferred from the system's operation or learned model parameters, even when considering potential side-channel attacks on FL updates.

5.  **Validate the Superiority over Generic Occupancy Prediction:** To empirically demonstrate that the proposed approach of inferring behavioral states leads to demonstrably better energy optimization outcomes and enhanced occupant comfort compared to state-of-the-art methods that rely solely on generic occupancy prediction. This involves rigorous comparative analysis under diverse operational scenarios.
    *   **Success Metric:** Statistical significance in improvements (e.g., p-value < 0.05) in energy savings and occupant satisfaction metrics when comparing the proposed FL-Transformer system against a reference system based on a high-accuracy FNN occupancy predictor.

## IV. Literature Review

The pursuit of intelligent energy management in built environments has a rich and evolving history, with significant research focusing on occupant detection and prediction as a cornerstone. Early approaches were largely reactive, relying on simple timers or basic occupancy sensors. The introduction of Passive Infrared (PIR) sensors marked a significant leap, allowing for more dynamic detection of human presence based on thermal differentials. These systems, however, were often prone to false positives and negatives due to factors like heat sources or slow movement. The subsequent integration of Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs) and temperature sensors provided richer contextual data, enabling rudimentary occupancy estimation by correlating changes in light levels and temperature with human presence.

The advent of machine learning revolutionized occupancy prediction. Studies like [1] explored the use of traditional algorithms such as Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and Random Forests for classifying occupancy states based on multimodal sensor data. These methods demonstrated improved accuracy over rule-based systems, achieving high precision in differentiating between occupied and unoccupied states. The paper [1] specifically highlights the potential of combining various sensor modalities to build more robust models, laying the groundwork for the multimodal data processing that is essential for our proposed framework. However, these methods often operated on aggregated data, losing the temporal dynamics crucial for inferring behavioral nuances.

More recent research has delved into deep learning architectures for occupancy prediction. Feedforward Neural Networks (FNNs) and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have shown promise in learning complex, non-linear relationships within sensor data. For instance, the work by [2] presents a hybrid TinyML-based system employing an FNN for predicting occupancy, achieving remarkable accuracy levels and demonstrating its integration into a dashboard for real-time decision-making and simulated energy control actions. This research is highly relevant as it establishes a benchmark for occupancy prediction accuracy and showcases the potential for ML-driven energy optimization. Nevertheless, the FNN-based approach, while effective for prediction, is inherently limited in its ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies and subtle sequential patterns within sensor streams. Such patterns are crucial for inferring the emergent states of "moods" and "activity intensities" that our hypothesis addresses. FNNs typically process fixed-size input vectors, making them less suitable for analyzing variable-length, sequential sensor data where the order and timing of events carry significant meaning.

Concerns regarding data privacy have also been a significant driver in research directions. Federated Learning (FL), introduced by Google in 2017, has emerged as a leading paradigm for privacy-preserving machine learning. FL allows models to be trained across decentralized edge devices holding local data samples, without exchanging these samples. Only model updates (e.g., gradients or trained model weights) are aggregated centrally. This approach has been explored for various applications, including keyboard prediction and healthcare [3]. However, its application to complex sensor data analysis for inferring subtle behavioral states in real-time building environments is still nascent. Existing FL applications often focus on simpler classification or regression tasks, and the adaptation of FL to sophisticated sequence modeling architectures like Transformers, especially for the nuanced task of behavioral inference, presents a novel challenge.

The Transformer architecture, originally developed for natural language processing, has demonstrated exceptional capabilities in sequence modeling due to its self-attention mechanism. This mechanism allows the model to weigh the importance of different parts of the input sequence, regardless of their position, making it highly effective for capturing long-range dependencies and contextual relationships. Recently, Transformers have been adapted for time-series analysis and anomaly detection in various domains, including industrial monitoring and financial forecasting. The application of Transformers to sensor stream analysis for detecting anomalies that signify behavioral shifts, as proposed in our hypothesis, is a novel extension. While [4] discusses the use of Transformers for time-series anomaly detection, its application to the specific context of inferring human behavioral states from anonymized building sensor data, particularly within a federated learning framework, remains an underexplored area. This gap highlights the novelty and potential impact of our proposed research.

Moreover, the existing literature often overlooks the direct link between inferred subtle behavioral states and energy optimization. While occupancy prediction directly informs energy control, the qualitative aspects of occupancy (moods, activity intensities) are largely ignored. This oversight leads to systems that are more energy-intensive than necessary and less comfortable for occupants. Our hypothesis aims to bridge this gap by establishing a direct causal link: inferring nuanced behavioral states enables more precise and contextually appropriate energy management strategies, thereby transcending the limitations of current, purely occupancy-centric approaches. The absence of research that combines FL, Transformer-based anomaly detection on anonymized sensor streams, and the inference of emergent behavioral "moods" and "activity intensities" for dynamic energy optimization signifies a critical research void that our work intends to fill.

## V. Methodology

The proposed methodology centers on a novel, privacy-preserving Federated Learning (FL) framework that leverages Transformer-based anomaly detection on anonymized sensor streams to infer localized, emergent behavioral states ("moods" and "activity intensities") within shared spaces. This inferred information is then utilized to drive dynamic, context-aware energy optimization strategies. The system is designed to operate in a highly decentralized manner, preserving user privacy while achieving advanced environmental control.

### A. Federated Learning Architecture for Distributed Training

At the core of our approach is a robust Federated Learning architecture. This architecture consists of multiple edge devices (e.g., microcontrollers embedded in sensors or local gateways) equipped with basic processing capabilities and a central server for model aggregation. Each edge device is responsible for collecting raw sensor data (PIR, LDR, temperature, humidity, CO2, etc.) from its local environment. Crucially, raw data is never transmitted to the central server. Instead, each edge device preprocesses its local data through an initial anonymization and feature extraction pipeline. This pipeline includes techniques such as differential privacy for noisy aggregation of local statistics or temporal discretization to obfuscate precise timing. Once processed, each edge device trains a local instance of the Transformer model using its anonymized sensor streams. Periodically, instead of transmitting data, each edge device sends its updated model parameters (e.g., weights and biases) to the central server. The central server then employs an aggregation algorithm, such as Federated Averaging (FedAvg), to combine these local model updates into a global model. This global model is then distributed back to the edge devices for the next round of training. This FL approach ensures that the learning process is distributed, and the central server only ever sees aggregated, non-identifiable model parameters, thereby guaranteeing a high level of privacy. The choice of FedAvg is motivated by its simplicity and effectiveness in achieving convergence for many deep learning tasks. However, we will also explore more advanced aggregation techniques like FedProx to handle heterogeneity in device capabilities and data distributions.

### B. Transformer-Based Anomaly Detection for Behavioral Inference

The heart of the inference engine is a Transformer model adapted for time-series anomaly detection on anonymized sensor streams. The Transformer's inherent capability to capture long-range dependencies and contextual relationships through its self-attention mechanism makes it exceptionally well-suited for analyzing the temporal patterns within sensor data that signify nuanced occupant behavior. For each edge device, the anonymized sensor stream for a given time window will be tokenized and fed into a Transformer encoder. The encoder consists of multiple self-attention layers and feed-forward networks. The self-attention mechanism allows the model to dynamically weigh the importance of different sensor readings at different time points within the sequence, enabling it to learn complex correlations. For instance, a subtle but sustained increase in PIR activity coupled with a gradual rise in ambient temperature and a decrease in ambient light, even if individually below significant thresholds, might indicate a shift from low activity to higher engagement.

The Transformer model will be trained in an unsupervised or semi-supervised manner to detect anomalies. During training, the model learns the normal patterns of sensor data that correspond to predictable states. Anomalies are then defined as deviations from these learned normal patterns. These anomalies are hypothesized to correlate with emergent behavioral states. Specifically:
*   **Activity Intensity:** An anomaly characterized by rapid fluctuations in PIR sensor data, potentially coupled with moderate changes in LDR and temperature, could signify high physical activity intensity. Conversely, prolonged periods of minimal PIR activation with stable environmental parameters might indicate low activity intensity.
*   **Behavioral "Moods":** Subtle, sustained shifts in multiple sensor modalities might correlate with collective "moods." For example, a gradual decrease in ambient light coupled with stable temperature and low PIR activity could, in a certain context, indicate a more relaxed or focused state, potentially interpreted as a "calm mood." Conversely, increased LDR fluctuations and moderate PIR activity might point towards a more energetic or perhaps even agitated state.

The Transformer will be configured with positional encodings to preserve the temporal order of sensor readings, and masked self-attention mechanisms will be used during training to prevent future information leakage. The output of the Transformer will be an anomaly score or a latent representation that captures the deviation from normalcy, which can then be interpreted to infer the behavioral state.

### C. Dynamic, Context-Aware Energy Optimization

The inferred behavioral states (activity intensity and mood) are translated into actionable commands for energy optimization. This is achieved through an intelligent control module that interfaces with the building's energy management system (BEMS). Instead of simply switching lights on/off based on occupancy, the system will modulate lighting intensity and color temperature based on the inferred mood and activity. For example, during periods of high activity intensity and potentially "energetic" moods, brighter, cooler lighting might be preferred. For periods inferring "calm" moods and low activity, dimmer, warmer lighting could be employed to save energy and enhance comfort.

Similarly, HVAC systems will be adjusted dynamically. High activity intensity, leading to increased occupant metabolic heat, will trigger increased ventilation rates and potentially a slight reduction in heating setpoints. Low activity intensity might allow for reduced ventilation and heating, leading to significant energy savings. The inferred "mood" could also influence thermal comfort preferences; for instance, a "calm" mood might tolerate a slightly wider temperature band than a "stressed" or "active" mood. The control module will employ a rule-based system informed by the anomaly detection outputs, or potentially a reinforcement learning agent that learns optimal control policies over time by observing the impact of its decisions on energy consumption and occupant feedback (if available). The system will be designed to learn optimal setpoints and control strategies specific to different zones within the building, creating a truly personalized yet privacy-preserving environmental control. This allows for proactive adjustments rather than reactive responses, minimizing energy waste by anticipating occupant needs rather than simply reacting to their presence.

### D. Anonymization and Privacy Guarantees

Privacy is a foundational principle of this research. The anonymization process begins at the edge device. Raw sensor data is processed locally. Techniques like:
*   **Temporal Aggregation:** Grouping sensor events within short time windows (e.g., 1-5 minutes) and reporting aggregated statistics (e.g., total PIR activations, average light level) rather than precise timestamps.
*   **Spatial Granularity:** Operating on sensor data from distinct zones or rooms, where re-identifying an individual based on sensor readings within a larger area is difficult.
*   **Differential Privacy:** Applying noise to local model updates or aggregated statistics before they are shared. This ensures that the contribution of any single data point to the final model is statistically indistinguishable from its absence.

Furthermore, the Federated Learning approach intrinsically protects privacy by ensuring that raw data never leaves the edge device. Only model parameters are shared. Advanced techniques will also be explored to mitigate potential privacy leakage from model updates themselves, such as secure aggregation protocols where the central server only receives the sum of model updates without seeing individual device contributions, and gradient masking to further obscure the information contained in gradients. The Transformer model, trained on anonymized data and learning emergent patterns, is designed to infer collective states, not individual identities. The absence of explicit user identifiers and the focus on aggregate behavioral signals are key to preventing profiling. Rigorous security audits and formal privacy analyses will be conducted to validate these guarantees.

## VI. Implementation

The implementation of the proposed Federated Learning (FL) framework for dynamic energy optimization in shared spaces involves a multi-faceted approach, spanning hardware deployment, software development, and integration with existing building infrastructure. The envisioned system is designed for edge deployment, ensuring low latency and minimal reliance on cloud connectivity for core operations.

### A. Edge Device Hardware and Sensor Integration

The foundation of the system comprises numerous edge devices strategically deployed within the shared space. Each edge device will be built around a low-power embedded system, such as a Raspberry Pi Zero W, an ESP32 microcontroller, or a dedicated TinyML accelerator board (e.g., Coral Dev Board). These devices will be equipped with a suite of environmental sensors. Primarily, this includes:
*   **Passive Infrared (PIR) Sensors:** To detect motion and presence by sensing thermal radiation. Multiple PIR sensors may be employed to cover different angles and detect activity patterns within a zone.
*   **Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs) / Ambient Light Sensors:** To measure ambient light levels, crucial for adjusting artificial lighting.
*   **Temperature and Humidity Sensors:** Standard components like DHT22 or BME280 to monitor thermal conditions.
*   **CO2 Sensors (e.g., MH-Z19):** To gauge indoor air quality, which is directly influenced by occupancy and activity levels, and is a key parameter for ventilation control.
*   **Optional: Acoustic Sensors:** Microphones can be used for detecting general sound levels and activity intensity, but would require strict privacy protocols and onboard processing for anonymization (e.g., only detecting sound pressure levels, not speech content).

The edge devices will be programmed to interface with these sensors, acquire data at a predefined sampling rate (e.g., every 10-30 seconds for most sensors, potentially higher for PIR). Each device will be responsible for local data buffering, initial anonymization, and running a local instance of the Transformer model. Connectivity for model update synchronization will be established via Wi-Fi or Ethernet, utilizing a lightweight communication protocol such as MQTT for efficient message brokering between edge devices and a central aggregator.

### B. Anonymization and Local Preprocessing Pipeline

Prior to any model processing or transmission, each edge device will implement a localized anonymization and preprocessing pipeline. This pipeline is critical for fulfilling our privacy guarantees.
1.  **Data Timestamping and Windowing:** Raw sensor readings are timestamped and grouped into fixed-duration temporal windows (e.g., 5-minute intervals). Precise timestamps will be obfuscated or relative within the window.
2.  **Statistical Aggregation:** Within each window, statistical features are computed. For PIR sensors, this might include the count of activation events, the duration of activations, and the average interval between activations. For LDR and temperature sensors, mean, median, variance, and rate of change over the window will be calculated.
3.  **Noise Injection (Optional but Recommended):** For critical parameters or where higher privacy assurance is needed, a small amount of random noise (drawn from a Laplace or Gaussian distribution with a controlled variance, adhering to differential privacy principles) can be added to aggregated statistics.
4.  **Feature Vector Creation:** The aggregated and potentially noisy statistical features from all sensors within a window are concatenated to form a feature vector. This vector represents a single time-step input to the Transformer model. The dimensionality of this vector will be determined by the number of sensors and the types of statistics computed.

### C. Federated Learning Framework Deployment

The FL framework will be orchestrated using established libraries and custom extensions.
1.  **Edge-Side Model Training:** Each edge device will host a local copy of the Transformer model. Frameworks like TensorFlow Lite or PyTorch Mobile will be used for efficient execution on embedded hardware. The Transformer model, designed for sequence analysis, will process sequences of these anonymized feature vectors. For example, it might process the last 6-12 feature vectors (representing 30-60 minutes of historical data) to predict the current state. The model will be trained using a distributed optimizer (e.g., FedAvg). The loss function will likely be an anomaly detection objective, such as Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the input and reconstructed input (in an autoencoder-like setup) or a margin-based loss for distinguishing normal from anomalous sequences.
2.  **Central Server for Aggregation:** A dedicated server (which can be an on-premises server or a secure cloud instance) will act as the central aggregator. It will receive model updates (weights and gradients) from all active edge devices. The FedAvg algorithm will be implemented here: receive model updates, compute the weighted average of these updates (weighted by the number of samples processed by each device), and send the global, updated model back to the edge devices. Secure communication protocols (e.g., TLS/SSL) will be employed for all data transfers.
3.  **Model Versioning and Synchronization:** The central server will manage model versions, ensuring that all edge devices are running the same global model during inference and using consistent versioning for updates.

### D. Transformer Model Architecture and Training for Anomaly Detection

The Transformer model itself will be architected to capture temporal dependencies effectively.
1.  **Input Embedding:** The anonymized feature vectors will be linearly projected into an embedding space. Positional encodings will be added to these embeddings to provide the model with information about the temporal order of the input sequence.
2.  **Transformer Encoder Layers:** A stack of Transformer encoder layers will process the embedded sequences. Each layer comprises a multi-head self-attention mechanism and a position-wise feed-forward network. The self-attention allows the model to learn relationships between different time steps in the input sequence. We will employ a number of encoder layers (e.g., 3-6) and attention heads (e.g., 4-8) as determined by hyperparameter tuning.
3.  **Anomaly Scoring Mechanism:** For anomaly detection, the Transformer can be structured as an encoder-decoder architecture (autoencoder) or as a predictive model. In an autoencoder setup, the decoder attempts to reconstruct the input sequence from the encoded representation. A high reconstruction error indicates an anomaly. Alternatively, a purely encoder-based approach can output latent representations that are then fed into a simpler anomaly detection layer (e.g., a Gaussian Mixture Model or a Support Vector Data Description) trained on normal data. The output will be an anomaly score for each time window.

Training will proceed in epochs, with each epoch involving a round of local training on edge devices and a global aggregation step. Initial training may require a period where devices have access to some labeled or semi-labeled data, or it will be fully unsupervised, learning from the inherent structure of normal operational data.

### E. Energy Optimization Control Module

The energy optimization control module resides on each edge device (or a local gateway for a cluster of devices) and acts upon the anomaly scores generated by the local Transformer model.
1.  **Inference and State Interpretation:** The local Transformer model processes the latest anonymized sensor sequence and outputs an anomaly score. This score is then interpreted to infer the current behavioral state:
    *   **Low Anomaly Score:** Indicates a "normal" or predictable state, managed by baseline occupancy prediction or standard settings.
    *   **Moderate to High Anomaly Score:** Signifies a deviation, suggesting a shift in "activity intensity" or "mood." The magnitude and pattern of the anomaly (e.g., rapid fluctuations vs. slow drifts) help distinguish between high/low activity and potentially different "moods." This interpretation can be done through predefined heuristics or a small, trained classifier mapping anomaly characteristics to state labels.
2.  **Dynamic Control Strategy:** Based on the inferred state, the control module adjusts parameters for lighting, HVAC, and ventilation. For instance:
    *   *High Activity Intensity:* Increase ventilation, potentially lower heating setpoint, adjust lighting to brighter/cooler.
    *   *Low Activity Intensity:* Reduce ventilation, potentially increase heating setpoint, adjust lighting to dimmer/warmer.
    *   *Inferred "Calm Mood":* Prioritize comfort, potentially warmer lighting, stable HVAC.
    *   *Inferred "Energetic Mood":* Brighter lighting, potentially cooler HVAC.
3.  **Integration with BEMS:** The control module will interact with the existing Building Energy Management System (BEMS) via standard protocols (e.g., BACnet, Modbus) to command actuators for lights, HVAC units, and dampers. Fallback mechanisms will be in place to revert to standard occupancy-based control or manual override in case of system failure or uninterpretable states.

This comprehensive implementation plan ensures that the proposed framework is not only theoretically sound but also practically feasible for deployment in real-world shared spaces, while rigorously upholding privacy and achieving advanced energy optimization.
