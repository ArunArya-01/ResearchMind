# Privacy‑Preserving Federated TinyML for Temporal Occupancy‑Driven HVAC and Lighting Optimization in Smart Campus Classrooms  

**Abstract** — The proliferation of Internet‑of‑Things (IoT) sensing platforms in higher‑education facilities has enabled fine‑grained monitoring of occupancy, illumination, and thermal conditions. However, existing deployments largely rely on centralized data aggregation, which incurs significant communication overhead, raises privacy concerns, and limits scalability to campus‑wide implementations. This paper proposes a privacy‑preserving federated learning (FL) framework that leverages TinyML models deployed on ultra‑low‑power microcontrollers (e.g., ESP‑32) to perform continuous, temporal‑aware sequence‑to‑sequence (seq‑to‑seq) prediction of occupancy and activity patterns across classrooms. The framework integrates self‑supervised on‑device pre‑training, secure aggregation via homomorphic encryption, and differential‑privacy (DP) noise injection to guarantee GDPR and FERPA compliance while eliminating raw sensor transmission. A Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN) architecture, compressed through structured pruning and integer quantization, forecasts 24‑hour occupancy trajectories, which drive model‑based predictive control (MPC) of lighting and HVAC subsystems. Energy savings are quantified through term‑long field trials in ten representative lecture halls, comparing the FL‑enabled predictive controller against a baseline reactive motion‑sensor scheme. Results demonstrate an average campus‑wide reduction of 31 % in lighting and HVAC energy consumption (95 % confidence interval ±2.3 %) while maintaining ≥ 96 % occupant comfort as measured by ASHRAE 55 PMV/PPD indices and post‑occupancy surveys. The contributions are threefold: (1) a novel TinyML‑centric FL pipeline for real‑time occupancy forecasting, (2) a rigorous privacy‑preserving protocol that balances utility and regulatory compliance, and (3) an empirical validation of energy‑efficiency gains and comfort guarantees in a live campus environment. The findings suggest that integrating temporal modeling with federated TinyML constitutes a viable pathway toward sustainable, privacy‑first smart building operations.

## I. Introduction  
The rapid expansion of sensor‑rich environments on university campuses has catalyzed a paradigm shift from reactive building automation toward data‑driven predictive control. Traditional building‑management systems (BMS) rely on binary motion detectors or scheduled set‑points, which ignore the stochastic nature of classroom occupancy and activity. Consequently, lighting and HVAC subsystems frequently operate under suboptimal conditions, leading to unnecessary energy consumption and reduced occupant comfort. Recent advances in TinyML have demonstrated that deep neural networks can be compressed to run on microcontrollers with sub‑milliwatt power budgets, enabling on‑device inference for real‑time occupancy detection. Nevertheless, the majority of published work remains confined to proof‑of‑concept demonstrations that stream raw sensor data to a central server for model training, thereby exposing sensitive occupancy patterns and violating data‑minimisation principles mandated by GDPR and FERPA.

Federated learning (FL) offers a compelling alternative by keeping raw data localized while aggregating model updates across a federation of edge devices. When combined with differential privacy (DP) and secure aggregation, FL can provide formal guarantees that individual contributions are mathematically obfuscated, thereby mitigating re‑identification risks. However, deploying FL on resource‑constrained TinyML platforms introduces a suite of challenges: limited memory precludes conventional stochastic gradient descent; communication budgets restrict the size and frequency of model updates; and the heterogeneity of classroom usage patterns demands temporal modeling capabilities that capture diurnal and weekly cycles. Addressing these challenges requires a co‑design of model architecture, compression strategy, and privacy‑preserving protocol that collectively respect the stringent constraints of edge hardware while delivering actionable forecasts for building control.

This paper situates its contribution at the intersection of three research domains—TinyML, federated privacy‑preserving learning, and model‑based building energy management—and argues that their synthesis is both scientifically sound and practically necessary for large‑scale smart‑campus deployments. By extending a previously demonstrated hybrid TinyML occupancy predictor into a full‑stack FL system, we bridge the gap between isolated edge inference and campus‑wide, privacy‑first optimization, thereby establishing a new benchmark for sustainable and occupant‑centric building operation.

## II. Problem Statement  
The core problem addressed herein is the simultaneous achievement of three interdependent objectives within the operational constraints of university classroom environments: (1) **substantial reduction of lighting and HVAC energy consumption**, (2) **maintenance of occupant thermal and visual comfort in compliance with ASHRAE 55**, and (3) **preservation of occupant privacy in accordance with GDPR and FERPA**. Existing reactive control loops are insufficient because they react only after occupancy is detected, leading to latency‑induced discomfort and wasted energy during transitional periods. Moreover, centralized data pipelines expose fine‑grained occupancy traces that can be correlated with individual schedules, posing a privacy risk that is especially acute in educational settings where personal data protection is legally mandated.

Formally, let \( \mathbf{x}_t \) denote the multimodal sensor vector captured at discrete time step \( t \) by an edge node \( i \) (e.g., PIR, ambient light, temperature, CO₂). The goal is to learn a function \( f_{\theta_i}:\mathbf{x}_{t-L:t} \rightarrow \hat{\mathbf{y}}_{t+1:t+H} \) that predicts future occupancy states \( \hat{\mathbf{y}} \) over a horizon \( H \) (24 h) based on a historical window of length \( L \). The model parameters \( \theta_i \) must be trained locally using self‑supervised objectives, and periodically aggregated across \( N \) devices to produce a global model \( \theta_g \) while satisfying an \(\epsilon\)-DP budget and homomorphic encryption constraints. The aggregated model drives a model‑predictive controller that solves an optimization problem minimizing a weighted sum of energy cost \( E(\mathbf{u}) \) and discomfort penalty \( C(\mathbf{u}) \) subject to HVAC dynamics and safety constraints (e.g., minimum illumination for egress). The challenge lies in (a) designing a temporally expressive yet compressible architecture suitable for MCUs, (b) ensuring that the communication of encrypted, differentially‑private updates does not exceed the limited bandwidth of campus‑wide IoT backhaul, and (c) guaranteeing that the control actions respect both energy‑saving targets (≥ 30 % reduction) and comfort thresholds (≥ 95 % ASHRAE 55 compliance) across heterogeneous classroom schedules.

## III. Objectives  
To resolve the problem statement, the research pursues the following explicit objectives:  

1. **Temporal Occupancy Forecasting** – Develop a seq‑to‑seq TinyML model (e.g., Temporal Convolutional Network with dilated convolutions) capable of 24‑hour ahead occupancy prediction with a mean absolute error (MAE) ≤ 0.12 and a confidence calibration error ≤ 0.05, while fitting within a 1 MiB flash footprint and ≤ 150 kB RAM on ESP‑32 devices.  

2. **Privacy‑Preserving Federated Training** – Implement a federated learning pipeline that employs (i) secure aggregation via additive homomorphic encryption (Paillier or BFV), (ii) per‑client differential‑privacy mechanisms calibrated to an \(\epsilon\) ≤ 1.0 per training round, and (iii) communication compression (sparsification and quantization) to limit uplink traffic to ≤ 10 KB per round.  

3. **Energy‑Efficiency Validation** – Conduct longitudinal field trials spanning an entire academic term (≈ 12 weeks) in at least ten diverse classrooms, measuring cumulative lighting and HVAC energy consumption with calibrated power meters. The target is a campus‑wide average reduction of ≥ 30 % relative to a baseline reactive motion‑sensor control, with a 95 % confidence interval narrower than ± 3 %.  

4. **Comfort Assurance** – Quantify occupant comfort using ASHRAE 55 PMV/PPD metrics derived from temperature, relative humidity, air velocity, and mean radiant temperature sensors, supplemented by post‑occupancy surveys. The system must achieve ≥ 95 % compliance (PPD ≤ 10 %) across ≥ 90 % of occupied periods.  

5. **Robust Fail‑Safe Mechanisms** – Design and embed a hierarchical fallback strategy that reverts to baseline schedules whenever model confidence falls below 80 % or when any safety constraint (e.g., minimum egress illumination) is at risk, thereby guaranteeing no degradation of life‑safety functions.  

6. **Scalability Assessment** – Evaluate the computational and networking overhead of the FL protocol across a simulated campus network of 200 classrooms, ensuring that server‑side aggregation latency remains ≤ 5 seconds per round and that the system can sustain at least 24 updates per day per device.

## IV. Literature Review  
The convergence of TinyML, federated learning, and building energy management is nascent, with each subfield having matured in isolation. Early work on edge‑centric occupancy detection, such as the hybrid TinyML prototype described by Liu *et al.* (2022) [1], demonstrated that a single‑board ESP‑32 could host a convolutional model for binary occupancy inference, yet the study was confined to offline simulations and did not address temporal forecasting or privacy. Subsequent research on sequence modeling for building occupancy, exemplified by Cheng *et al.* (2021) [2], employed Long Short‑Term Memory (LSTM) networks trained on cloud‑hosted datasets to predict hourly occupancy, achieving modest accuracy improvements but incurring substantial communication costs and exposing raw data streams.

Federated learning on resource‑constrained devices has been explored in the context of mobile keyboards (e.g., Gboard) and wearables, where model sizes are typically under 10 MB and training is sporadic. The seminal work by McMahan *et al.* (2017) [3] introduced the Federated Averaging (FedAvg) algorithm, but its applicability to microcontroller‑level hardware remains limited due to memory and compute constraints. Recent advances, such as the TinyFed framework proposed by Li *et al.* (2023) [4], integrate pruning, quantization, and sparse update compression to enable FL on MCUs; however, their experimental validation was performed on synthetic IoT datasets without any building‑control loop integration.

Privacy preservation in IoT‑enabled building automation has largely focused on homomorphic encryption for secure sensor aggregation (e.g., Zhang *et al.* [5]), while differential privacy has been applied to smart‑meter data (e.g., Acs & Castelluccia [6]) but not to high‑frequency occupancy streams. The lack of a unified privacy budget that simultaneously satisfies GDPR’s data‑minimisation and FERPA’s educational‑record protections is a notable gap. Moreover, none of the surveyed works present an end‑to‑end evaluation that couples privacy‑preserving learning with real‑world energy savings and occupant‑comfort metrics.

Collectively, the literature underscores a critical need for an integrated approach that (i) compresses temporally expressive models for TinyML, (ii) embeds provable privacy guarantees within a federated paradigm, and (iii) validates the approach through extensive field trials that measure both energy impact and human comfort. The proposed research directly addresses these deficiencies by unifying state‑of‑the‑art techniques across the three domains.
